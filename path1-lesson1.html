Types of Banks: Online vs. In-Person

Types of Banks: Online vs. In-Person

The Situation

You need a bank account, but you're not sure which type is right for you

Why It Matters

Picking the right bank makes managing your money way easier

What You'll Learn

The difference between online and traditional banks, and how to actually use them

Online Banks: Everything Happens on Your Phone or Computer

So what even is an online bank?

It's exactly what it sounds like. You do everything through an app or website. No physical building to walk into, no tellers behind a counter. Your account lives entirely online.

The Good Stuff:

  • Better interest rates: Online banks don't pay for fancy buildings, so they pass those savings on to you with higher interest on savings accounts.
  • Lower fees: Many online banks have zero monthly fees. Seriously, zero.
  • Super convenient: Check your balance, transfer money, deposit checks (yep, with your phone camera) whenever you want, wherever you are.
  • Fast setup: You can open an account in like 10 minutes from your couch.

The Not-So-Great Stuff:

  • No physical location: You can't walk in and talk to someone face to face if something goes wrong.
  • Cash deposits are tricky: You can't just hand someone cash. You'd need to use an ATM or workaround methods.
  • Learning curve: If you're not comfortable with apps and websites, it might feel overwhelming at first.

Who it's best for: People who are comfortable with tech, rarely need to deposit cash, and want to save on fees.

Traditional Banks: The Places with Physical Buildings

These are the banks you see on street corners.

You know, the ones with the big signs and actual buildings you can walk into. Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, your local credit union. These are traditional banks.

The Good Stuff:

  • Face-to-face help: If something goes wrong or you're confused, you can literally walk in and talk to a person.
  • Easy cash deposits: Got cash? Just walk in and hand it to the teller. Done.
  • ATMs everywhere: Physical branches mean more ATMs, and they're usually free to use for customers.
  • Familiar and trusted: Some people just feel more comfortable seeing the building and knowing it's been around for years.

The Not-So-Great Stuff:

  • Higher fees: Those buildings and staff cost money, so they charge more for accounts and services.
  • Lower interest rates: They don't pay you as much interest on your savings.
  • Less convenient hours: You're stuck with their business hours. Need to do something at 9 PM? Tough luck.
  • More paperwork: Opening an account in person can take longer and require more documents.

Who it's best for: People who like in-person service, need to deposit cash regularly, or just prefer the traditional way of banking.

How to Actually Navigate a Bank Website

Okay, so you picked a bank. Now what? Bank websites can feel confusing if you've never used one. Here's what to look for:

Opening an Account:

  • Look for buttons that say "Open an Account" or "Get Started" (usually at the top of the page)
  • You'll pick between checking or savings first
  • They'll ask for your name, address, social security number, and ID
  • Some need a minimum deposit (like $25), others don't

What to Expect on the Homepage:

  • "Personal" or "Personal Banking" - That's where you want to be (not business accounts)
  • "Checking" and "Savings" - Links to account info
  • "Login" or "Sign In" - Top right corner, that's how you access your account after you open it
  • "Contact Us" or "Help" - If you get stuck, this is your friend

Once You're In Your Account:

  • Dashboard: Shows your balance, recent transactions
  • "Transfer" or "Move Money": Send money between accounts or to other people
  • "Deposit": Upload a check photo (seriously, it's that easy)
  • "Statements": Monthly summaries of your account activity

Don't overthink it. Banks want you to use their site, so they try to make it simple. If you get lost, just call them.

So Which One Should You Pick?

Honestly, it depends on your life. Here's a quick guide:

Go with an Online Bank if:

  • You're comfortable with apps and websites
  • You rarely deal with cash
  • You want to save money on fees
  • You want better interest rates on savings
  • You like doing things on your own time

Popular options: Ally Bank, Chime, Marcus by Goldman Sachs, Capital One 360

Go with a Traditional Bank if:

  • You need to deposit cash regularly
  • You prefer talking to someone face to face
  • You like having a physical location nearby
  • You're not super comfortable with technology
  • You want ATMs everywhere

Popular options: Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, local credit unions

Real talk: You can always switch banks later. This isn't a forever decision. A lot of people actually use both—an online bank for savings (better interest) and a traditional bank for everyday stuff. Do what feels right for you.
